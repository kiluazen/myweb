If a "what people are up to with their agents" network is going to matter, the people it serves have to be growing. They are. This is the macro tailwind, with numbers, and an honest accounting of where the hype outruns the data.

## The structural baseline

The one-person business is not new and not a fad. US **nonemployer firms** (businesses with no employees beyond the owner) grew from 22.7 million in 2012 to **30.4 million in 2023**, a 34% rise over eleven years. They averaged 2.7% annual growth versus 1.1% for employer firms over that period (Census Bureau, "The Steady Rise of the Nonemployer Business," July 2025).

Two caveats keep this honest: nonemployers are ~79% of US businesses by count but generate only **$1.8 trillion in receipts, about 6.4% of GDP**. The trend is real; the revenue ceiling on solo work is also real.

The post-pandemic surge is the new part. The US logged ~5.7 million business applications in 2025, and 2026 opened with the strongest five-month start in the history of the Business Formation Statistics, with March 2026 setting a single-month record.

## The Stripe signal

Stripe Economics published "The Age of the Solopreneur" on June 22, 2026 (Tedeschi, Rama, Cruickshank). It is payment data, not a survey, which is what makes it interesting. Their proxy index spans ~115 solopreneur platforms plus Stripe Atlas incorporations. The headline findings:

- **More than twice as many solopreneurs earned over $1M in 2025 than in 2023.**
- **Close to three times as many crossed $5M and $10M.**
- The 2025 sign-up cohort reached $1M in cumulative revenue ~30% faster than the 2023 cohort, and roughly **3x faster than the 2019 cohort**.
- AI-influenced sign-ups are ~4x the share they were a year earlier, and Stripe calls the 20% it can explicitly measure "a floor, not a ceiling."

International registration data rules out a US-only artifact: new business registrations are up ~40% in Australia, ~70% in Finland, and ~80% in France since 2017, the last mostly solo founders.

## The longitudinal check

MBO Partners has run the longest annual survey of independent workers (15 years). Their 2025 report: **5.6 million Americans earned over $100K/year as independents, up 19% in a year, nearly double 2020 (3M), nearly triple 2011 (1.9M).** And **74% of independents now use AI tools**, reporting an average of 9 hours saved per week. Survey data carries selection bias, but a 14-year tripling is a durable slope, not a blip.

## What it looks like in the wild

- **Medvi** (Matthew Gallagher): GLP-1 telehealth, launched Sept 2024 on $20K. 2025 revenue **$401M, ~$65M net profit, 250,000 customers, two people**. AI stack across code, copy, and early support.
- **Base44** (Maor Shlomo): AI app builder, built solo in ~4 months, ~$1.5M first-month revenue, **acquired by Wix for $80M** about six months post-launch.
- **HeadshotPro** (Danny Postma): ~$300K/month, solo, from Bali.

The honest asterisk: these are the top of a wide distribution. The average solopreneur takes home ~$39K and **36% earn under $25K**. Medvi works partly because GLP-1 is a once-in-a-generation demand wave, not because AI alone makes any telehealth business a two-person shop.

## Structural or hype?

The most defensible read: a **real, durable shift in the lower bound** of what one person can build and earn, with AI compressing the time to get there. The $100M+ solo outliers are anomalies; the durable signal is the six-figure cohort tripling over 14 years across three independent data streams (Census applications, Stripe payments, MBO survey) pointing the same way at once.

Against over-extrapolation: nonemployers still earn under 7% of business revenue; the median has not moved proportionally; and Upwork demand for writing (-32% YoY), IT (-27%), and web dev (-13%) shows AI hollowing out the lower tier even as it amplifies the top. Average team size at sub-one-year-old companies has drifted to 3-4 people (J.P. Eggers, NYU Stern) as AI absorbs early hires, but compliance, physical supply chains, and enterprise sales still resist soloing.

## Why it matters for a builder network

If more people can build a real business alone, the old Indie Hackers question gets sharper: how do solo operators get discovered, trusted, compared, copied, and remembered when there is no company headcount trail? A growing population of capable solo builders is exactly the demand pool a builder network needs, provided the network surfaces the part that has stakes, decisions, money, and lessons, not a feed of activity.
