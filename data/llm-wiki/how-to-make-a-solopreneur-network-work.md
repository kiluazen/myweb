If the craving is real (high confidence) and the audience is growing (high confidence), the only question left is how you actually build one that lasts. The operator pattern from Courtland Allen (Indie Hackers), Ryan Hoover (Product Hunt), Pieter Levels (Nomad List), Marc Köhlbrugge (WIP), and Rosie Sherry (Ministry of Testing) is consistent enough to use, but the exact playbook still needs live testing for an agent-native network.

## The one rule under all the others

Every community that survived more than three years was built around a **useful object** — something a member needed whether or not anyone else showed up. The community was the bonus, not the product. WIP's todo list, Ministry of Testing's conference recordings, Nomad List's city data, Indie Hackers' revenue-backed interviews. The ones that made the feed itself the product mostly decayed.

Courtland Allen is blunt about it: **90% of Indie Hackers traffic went to the interviews, not the forum.** "Indie Hackers in its first month was a blog." The interviews (real numbers, real stakes) were the object; the forum was the network-effect layer bolted on top.

## The principles that transfer

**1. Useful object first.** Build the thing people need for their work before the social layer. For an agent network, the object is the *agent log / deployment record / what-it-earned*, not a post. People come to track their own work; community happens around the artifact. *(Köhlbrugge, Sherry)*

**2. Paid is a relevancy filter, not revenue.** Levels raised Nomad List "$5, then $25, then $50, then $99" in direct response to spam. Köhlbrugge framed payment as a relevancy filter for WIP. The safer rule: charge or approve publishing once there is a reason to protect quality; do not charge before the private utility is real. *(Levels, Köhlbrugge; principle high-confidence, exact price low-confidence)*

**3. Stories with stakes beat activity feeds.** A generic "what I did today" post has no visible stakes, so nobody reads it and nobody posts. Revenue transparency is what made Indie Hackers credible. Surface "what this person built with their agents this month, what changed, and what proof exists" — real numbers are best, but external proof can also work. *(Allen; high-confidence principle)*

**4. A time-bounded ritual is a durable mechanic.** Product Hunt's daily reset created a return habit; the 7:30am email, same time, same shape, embedded it in mornings. "By time-bounding what we're doing, we frame Product Hunt as something people set aside time to do each day." A daily/weekly "what shipped" digest competes with email, not with other feeds. *(Hoover)*

**5. Seed before you open. Never launch a ghost town.** Hoover hand-picked 10-15 curators; Allen literally posted fake discussion threads for a year until the forum hit critical mass; Graham seeded HN himself. Recruit 20-30 active builders by DM and curate the first 50 logs yourself. *(Hoover, Allen)*

**6. The streak is accountability, not gamification.** It works only when attached to a real behavior with stakes (shipping), not a hollow metric. Levels: "I felt accountability because I put it public." A public agent-run log with a streak beats likes and upvotes. *(Levels, Köhlbrugge)*

**7. Niche identity over broad audience.** Hoover: "If Product Hunt was for everything, no one would gravitate to it, because they want to self-identify with it." Sherry built for *software testers*, not "developers." "People building with AI agents" is too broad; "indie builders running agents that earn money" is narrow enough to become an identity. *(Hoover, Sherry)*

**8. Start without a platform, and don't over-build.** Sherry: "Don't use a community tool until it feels so painful that what you're doing doesn't work." She ran on email, then Ning for nine years. Hoover started on a Linkydink email list. And once culture is fragile, don't bury it in features: "members don't care if the upvote button looks shinier." *(Sherry, Hoover)*

**9. IRL resets the relationship when online plateaus.** Ministry of Testing's revenue breakthrough was TestBash. "When you organize an event, everybody knows you." One annual "show your agents" meetup will do more for retention than six months of forum tuning. *(Sherry)*

## The failure modes (aggregated)

- **Ghost-town launch** — opening before manufacturing activity.
- **Feed without stakes** — "what I did today" with no numbers, streak, or artifact.
- **Free forever** — no filter, so lurkers consume and never contribute.
- **Broadening too fast** — Product Hunt's gaming/books expansion before the core was locked.
- **Over-tooling early** — elaborate Circle/Discourse installs before a community exists.
- **Feature bloat** — redesigns that disrupt established behavior.
- **Selling to members** — turning the room into a marketing channel; the quality contributors leave first.
- **Broadcasting into the void** — WIP's 2024 critique: a todo object with no response loop still fails.

## The opening nobody has taken

Here is the part worth sitting with: **there is no dedicated community yet for "what I'm building with my AI agents" in any structured form.** The space is served by Twitter build-in-public threads and ad hoc Discords — high noise, no useful object, no paid filter, no streak or status mechanic. Every ingredient the operators name as essential is missing.

So the recipe, if you wanted to build it:

1. The **object** is the agent log (what ran, what shipped, what it changed), not a post.
2. **Private utility first**, then paid/approved publishing as a quality filter.
3. The unit people read is a **story with a number**, not a feed of activity.
4. A **weekly "what shipped" digest** as the time-bound ritual.
5. **Seed it by hand** with 20-30 real builders before opening.
6. A **streak** on the real behavior (agent runs that produced something).
7. Narrow the identity: builders whose agents make money.
8. Start in a group chat; earn the platform later.
